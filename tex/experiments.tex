\chapter{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

In this chapter, we present the experimental parameters and performance results based on the framework and downstream applications described in the previous chapter. We compare the raw CSI baseline with four distinct representations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CLS Embeddings:} The global representation vector extracted from the LWM transformer encoder.
    \item \textbf{Channel Embeddings:} The token-level spatial-frequency representation.
    \item \textbf{AE - 1/16:} A compressed vector $\mathbf{z}$ generated by an Autoencoder with a 1:16 compression ratio.
    \item \textbf{AE - 1/32:} A compressed vector $\mathbf{z}$ generated by an Autoencoder with a 1:32 compression ratio.
\end{itemize}

Furthermore, for the CLS representation, we compare the performance of the frozen backbone against partial fine-tuning (the last three layers) and full fine-tuning.

Since one of the primary goals of this work is to demonstrate that the compressed vector $\mathbf{z}$ can be used efficiently for downstream tasks, we only utilized the encoder portion of the autoencoder during evaluation. All calculated inference times, parameter counts, and floating-point operations (FLOPs) strictly reflect this architectural choice.

For all downstream tasks, synthetic data was generated using real-life scenarios from the DeepMIMO dataset \cite{Alkhateeb2019}. The Denver scenario (\texttt{city\_18\_denver}) features 3 available BSs and 6,970 User Equipments (UEs) uniformly distributed across the user grid. These maps, illustrated in \ref{fig:scenario_maps}, show the urban topology and the coverage map for a selected BS. Because this specific scenario was not seen by the LWM or the Autoencoders during their initial pretraining, it serves as an excellent benchmark to showcase the models' generalization capabilities in a standard urban environment.

The dataset generation uses a standardized configuration across all scenarios to ensure a consistent comparison. At the BS, a ULA is employed, consisting of 32 antenna elements with a predefined rotation angle of $-135^\circ$ in the horizontal plane. On the user side, every UE is equipped with a single antenna. Furthermore, the ray-tracing engine computes 20 distinct multipath components for every channel link.


In the frequency domain, the system employs Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing (OFDM) with 32 active subcarriers and a subcarrier spacing of 30 kHz. Additionally, in the Denver simulation, we activate all three BSs and filter out any samples that lacked a valid path between the UE and the BS. However, for the specific downstream task of power allocation, UEs were grouped strictly within the coverage area of their respective serving BS.

Once the channel matrices $\mathbf{H}$ between each user and the selected BSs are generated, the complex coefficients are multiplied by a scaling factor of $\alpha = 10^{6}$. This uniform scaling strategy was adopted to increase training stability and ensure that the input magnitudes align with the distribution expected by the pretrained LWM model. This adjustment minimizes the risk of vanishing gradients and allows for more stable downstream fine-tuning. For all experiments, the dataset was strictly partitioned into 60\% for training, 20\% for validation, and 20\% for testing.

For every experiment, we systematically evaluate the learned representations across three dimensions: data efficiency, noise robustness, and computational complexity. Furthermore, we perform an analysis of the latent space through t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) visualizations \cite{vandermaaten2008visualizing}. The goal of this qualitative analysis is to demonstrate that the pretrained models are capable of inherently capturing channel conditions and spatial features within their latent manifolds.

Additionally, we measured the number of parameters and FLOPs utilized by the LWM and the Autoencoders. As summarized in \ref{tab:encoder_complexity}, despite requiring fewer overall FLOPs, the LWM exhibits a higher memory footprint than the Autoencoders. This discrepancy is explained by the fundamental architectural differences between the sequential, highly parallelizable operations of CNNs and the memory-bandwidth-bound self-attention mechanisms of Transformers.

Finally, all training loops and benchmark tests were conducted using an NVIDIA L4 GPU provisioned through Google Colab.

\section{Line-of-Sight (LoS) Classification Results}\label{sec:experiments_los_nlos}

The first set of experiments focuses on the binary classification of LoS and NLoS conditions.

For this downstream task, we utilized the downstream model (or Task Head) described in Section \ref{sec:task_los_nlos_model} with a hidden dimension equal to 256. As stated in the previous chapter, the input is the flattened output of the Autoencoder, the flattened LWM embeddings, or the raw channel matrix. Since this is a binary classification task, the final output size is always 2. Because of the varying input dimensions, the number of parameters and FLOPs of the downstream model depends heavily on the chosen representation.

\subsection{Data Efficiency}\label{sec:experiments_los_nlos_data_eff}

To evaluate the data efficiency of different representations, we trained the downstream MLP using a wide range of training volumes. Specifically, we sampled from 1\% of the training pool, which corresponds to 23 samples in the Denver dataset, up to 100\% of the available training data (2,355 samples).

\begin{figure}[htbp]

\end{figure}

The \ref{fig:data_eff_los_nlos_denver} shows that the learned representations perform well in this scenario, with the Channel Embeddings demonstrating excellent performance in the few-shot regime. These representations saturate significantly earlier than the Raw CSI baseline. However, given enough data, all representations eventually converge to similar performance levels.

\begin{figure}[htbp]

These results demonstrate a clear advantage in low-data environments, where the representations learned by the LWM and the Autoencoders significantly outperform the baseline. Nevertheless, while the global CLS representation can struggle in certain extreme low-data scenarios, its performance can be vastly improved through fine-tuning as seen in \ref{fig:data_eff_fine_tune_los_nlos_denver}.

This counterintuitive behavior likely occurs because the model overfits to the clean representations.

\subsection{Latent Space Analysis}\label{sec:experiments_los_nlos_latent}

To understand the underlying mechanisms driving the superior data efficiency and noise robustness of the learned representations, we conducted a qualitative analysis using t-SNE. By projecting the high-dimensional embeddings into a 2D space, we can visualize how the models intrinsically cluster the channel conditions.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{experiments/los_nlos/tsne_comparison.pdf}
    \caption{Latent space visualizations for the evaluated representations. The figure is complemented by the scenario layout.}
    \label{fig:tsne_comparison_data_los_nlos_denver}
\end{figure}

As seen in \ref{fig:tsne_comparison_data_los_nlos_denver}, the t-SNE projections provide compelling visual evidence to support the preceding quantitative analyses:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{Raw CSI} exhibits highly overlapping regions, which fundamentally complicates channel state classification for a simple downstream MLP.
    \item The \textbf{Autoencoders} demonstrate a greater degree of spatial separation, resulting in fewer overlapping boundaries between the classes.
    \item Even without fine-tuning, the \textbf{frozen LWM} exhibits an incredible degree of natural clustering, which explains the high data efficiency of this representation in the few-shot regime.
    \item After \textbf{full fine-tuning}, the embedding space becomes remarkably segregated. The LoS and NLoS conditions are almost perfectly linearly separated. For this reason, the downstream classification head is able to achieve a near-perfect F1-Score with only a trivial volume of training data.
\end{itemize}

Under noisy conditions, the Raw CSI remains the most robust representation. The CLS representation's performance collapses even in the least complex scenarios. The compressed vector $\mathbf{z}$ demonstrates the strongest robustness among the learned representations; however, in extremely noisy conditions, it still lags behind the baseline. The channel embeddings show good results under low complexity and high SNR, but when one of these variables changes, the performance collapses rapidly.

\subsection{Latent Space Analysis}\label{sec:experiments_beam_latent}

To further understand the underlying representations, we performed a qualitative analysis through the visualization of the latent space using t-SNE. The \ref{fig:tsne_beam_selection} shows the behavior of the models for the selection problem using 16 beams. The data points are colored by their optimal beam index.


\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{experiments/beam_selection/tsne_comparison.pdf}
    \caption{t-SNE visualizations of the latent space for the beam selection task. The points are colored by their optimal beam index, illustrating the degree of spatial-feature entanglement across different representations.}
    \label{fig:tsne_beam_selection}
\end{figure}

The visual evidence strongly corroborates the performance trends observed in the preceding analysis:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{CLS embeddings} have a highly entangled latent space. The different beam classes overlap heavily. This is expected because the global token was designed to capture macroscopic channel properties; it lacks the fine-grained magnitude and phase information necessary for this precise directional task.
    \item \textbf{Fine-tuning} completely transforms the latent space and creates a clear separation between different classes, which explains the performance gains over the frozen model.
    \item \textbf{Channel embeddings} produce an entangled latent space. However, their downstream classification performance is satisfactory, which indicates that the high-dimensional embeddings successfully extract the necessary spatial information even if it is not linearly separable in 2D space.
    \item The \textbf{Autoencoders} show moderate clustering, resulting in good performance compared to the LWM embeddings. This indicates that the convolutional network successfully compresses the channel state information while retaining the crucial spatial structure.
    \item While the \textbf{Raw Channel} does not exhibit natural clustering in the t-SNE projection, the data natively retains all the necessary physical information for beam selection, explaining its superior quantitative results.
\end{itemize}

\section{Power Allocation Results}\label{sec:experiments_power}

This task aims to evaluate if the learned representations are useful for tasks where it is necessary to manage the inter-user interference. For all the learned representations, we used the downstream model described on \ref{sec:task_power_model} with 256 hidden channels in the entry block and the two residual blocks. The input size depends on the representation and the output size is equal to the number of users, which is the complexity variable for this task.

For this task, we did not compare the fine-tuning because in every case it did not bring significant difference in the experiments.

\subsection{Data Efficiency}\label{sec:experiments_power_data_eff}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering

    \caption{Data efficiency results for Power Allocation in the Denver scenario.}
    \label{fig:power_allocation_data_eff}

During the tests, it was clear that the autoencoder representation had a better performance. Even in low-data volumes, it showed near optimal performance. At 2 and 4 users, the raw channels baseline did not match the autoencoder even with 100\% of the dataset. Both LWM representations had unsatisfactory results. They failed to improve as the volume of data increases and had an unstable training.

As the number of users increases, the theoretical upper bound for the sum-rate improves. This phenomenon happens because the base station has a greater user diversity to choose from. When the pool increases, the system has a higher probability of finding a set of users that has good channel conditions and low interference. For those reasons, the gap between different representations also vanishes. This condition will happen until the number of users becomes the same as the number of antennas in the BS.

It is important to notice that the performance of all representations degrades as the channel conditions worsen. However, this fact is masked because the upper and lower bounds also degrade.

The analysis at \ref{fig:tsne_power_allocation} shows that it is difficult to organize the power budget only through spatial segregation in the latent space. All the representations have a highly entangled space. The raw channel baseline reveals complete absence of organization.

This demonstrates that the power allocation is a more complex task. Since the output depends on the interference between users, the representation is not enough to predict the allocated power. For this reason, the task demanded a more powerful downstream model.
